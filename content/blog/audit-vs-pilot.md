Every travel operator I talk to this year has the same opening line: "we tried a pilot, it didn't really go anywhere."

Here's why.

## A pilot tests a hypothesis

Pilots are great when you already know what you want to build. You have a specific workflow in mind, a specific tool, a specific user — and you want to know if it works in your context. That's useful.

The problem: most travel operators don't have a hypothesis worth testing yet. They have a vague sense that "AI could help somewhere" and pick a pilot based on what a vendor showed them at a conference.

## An audit tells you which hypothesis is worth testing

An audit looks at your actual operation — office ops, field ops, handoffs, bottlenecks, tribal knowledge — and maps where AI genuinely moves the needle. Output is a prioritized list of systems to build, in what order, with estimated impact and effort for each.

Now you have a hypothesis. Run the pilot on item #1.

## The pattern

- **Audit first**: 2 weeks, $3K–$6K, deliverable is a playbook you own
- **Pilot second**: 2–6 weeks, build the top playbook item, measure, iterate
- **Roll out**: the rest of the playbook, on whatever timeline fits your team

This feels like more process. It's actually less — because you skip the six months of "we piloted a chatbot and it didn't stick."

## What to look for in an audit

Any audit worth its price should:

1. Interview your actual guides, ops staff, and leadership — not just founders
2. Map your current tools and where the handoffs break
3. Come out with specific recommendations (not "use AI for customer service")
4. Include effort/impact estimates so you can prioritize
5. Be vendor-neutral — if the audit recommends a specific $50K platform in step one, ask why

## TL;DR

If you've already tried a pilot and it didn't go anywhere, the pilot wasn't wrong. The starting place was. Skip back, audit your actual operation, pick the pilot that matters, and run *that* one.
